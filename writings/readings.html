2020

  1. 1984 by George Orwell
    • The actions of the CCP and the USA increasingly resemble the themes of this dystopian narrative. An important reminder of the repercussions that my work can have on society.
  2. Animal Farm by George Orwell
    • Greed is an inherent part of human nature. Vigilance is an obligation.
  3. Talking to Strangers by Malcolm Gladwell
    • "The right way to talk to strangers is with caution and humility."
  4. The Rape of Nanking by Iris Chang
    • A nightmare inducing yet sobering read on the atrocities committed by the Japanese in Nanking.

2019

  1. 12 Rules For Life: An Antidote to Chaos by Jordan Peterson
    • A reminder on the importance of personal responsibility in navigating life. I found rules 6 (Set your house in perfect order before you criticize the world) and 7 (Pursue what is meaningful (not what is expedient)) to be most applicable to my life.
  2. Just Mercy by Bryan Stevenson
    • Highlights the unfortunate flaws of our criminal justice system. Profoundly moving.

2018

  1. The Grand Inquisitor by Fyodor Dostoevsky
    • Commitment to a higher purpose is a conscious decision.

2016

  1. I Am Malala by Malala Yousafzai
    • A reminder of the tremendous privilege that I come from. There is a lot of work left to do in the realm of women's rights.
  2. The Wonderful Wizard of Oz by L. Frank Baum
    • A light yet colorful read. Led me to discover The Wiz: a musical reimagining of the narrative featuring an entirely African American cast.
  3. Uncle Tom's Cabin by Harriet Beecher Stowe
    • The yin and yang relationship between Augustine St. Clare and his twin brother Alfred St. Clare echoes the relationship between myself and my older sister. Although we are fundamentally different, our differences are neither bad nor good.

2015

  1. The Catcher in the Rye by J.D. Salinger
    • The transition from adolescence to adulthood can be challenging to reconcile but is also an inevitable part of the human condition. Internal conflict is a part of the growing process.
  2. The Great Gatsby by F. Scott Fitzgerald
    • Creating a false persona to bolster one's perceived image provides little value in the long term. Lies always find a way of manifesting themselves.
  3. The Reader by Bernhard Schlink
    • Past conflict can be difficult to reconcile. Forgiveness is the ultimate virtue.

2014

  1. Night by Elie Wiesel
    • This book exposed me to the atrocities of the 20th century and the reality of the suffering that we are all capable of inflicting on others.
  2. Outliers by Malcolm Gladwell
    • Exceptional people come from exceptional circumstances. This book taught me the importance of analyzing things from a multivariate perspective.
  3. The Heart of Darkness by Joseph Conrad
    • Darkness lies where you least expect it.

2013

  1. Leonardo by Maria Constantino & Aileen Reid
  2. Michelangelo by Lucinda Hawkins Collinge & Annabel Ricketts
    • Both Leonardo and Michelangelo were end of the year gifts from my high school World History teacher. I attribute the beginning of my fascination towards Western art and culture to these books.

2011

  1. The Outsiders by S.E. Hinton
    • "Stay gold, Ponyboy, stay gold.".
